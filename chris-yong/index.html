About Thrive

Who they are

Thrive is the operating name of the Community Council for Somerset — a charity with over 100 years of history (2026 is their centenary year). Registered at Fitzwarren House, Queen Street, Taunton. Charity number 1069260.

They have historically operated through contracts with Somerset County Council and are now actively repositioning — moving from contract delivery toward a more mission-focused model with growing unrestricted fundraising income. This is a genuine and significant pivot.

Their belief: everyone in Somerset should be able to live a healthy, independent and safe life and be part of a thriving community.

What they do — services and the village agent model

Thrive's work covers three areas: individuals and families (debt advice, fuel poverty support, mental health signposting); community buildings and places (training for people running local community spaces); and diverse and displaced communities (support for minority ethnic communities and refugees).

Their 70 village agents are paid staff — not volunteers — embedded across Somerset. They visit people at home, run talking cafes, provide advice and crisis support, and connect people to services. The FM's job is strategic enablement — building fundraising culture with the agents, designing toolkits, creating data capture systems — not operational delivery at events.

The fundraising picture

Thrive's income is almost entirely statutory contracts and grants. A very small number of existing regular givers. The real gap is donor acquisition. The centenary goal is 2,000 actively interested supporters by year end — not donors yet. 100 regular givers by year end would be welcome but is not a hard target.

There is no fixed acquisition budget. Every spend must be proposed with a clear rationale: channel, likely response, cost per supporter, break-even at regular giving level. Email lists exist (a few thousand contacts) but are not consented for fundraising — Thrive must not fundraise from current clients. Beacon CRM is in place.

Culture, values and the growth trajectory

Thrive genuinely lives its values: kindness and compassion, honesty and transparency, quality and diversity. Family-friendly, flexible, and an environment where it is okay to take risks and fail. Increasingly mission-focused — frontline staff will notice immediately whether a fundraiser genuinely cares.

If the FM performs well in year one, a digital fundraising officer follows in year two and the FM grows into head of fundraising. This is a builder role with a genuine career trajectory.

Why This Role — Preparing Your Answers
Why do you want this job?

Lead with the build. You have spent nearly eight years managing a programme at Hft — and for much of that time, managing managed decline. You have had the skills for more, but the organisation has not had the scope to use them. You want to build something from the ground up, in a context where the proposition is genuinely compelling and the community impact is real.

The opportunity to build Thrive's individual giving and community fundraising programme from near-zero — using the centenary as a platform, with 70 village agents as the infrastructure — is exactly the kind of challenge you want. Not a maintenance role. A builder role.

You are not leaving Hft for a better salary or a better title. You are leaving because this role offers something Hft cannot: the scope to build a fundraising movement in a community you understand and care about.

Why Thrive specifically?

You described Thrive as 'hyperlocal, preventative in terms of the work they do, human, very practical — it centres itself around people rather than services.' That is not a description from the job specification. That is your own analysis. Use it.

You connected Thrive to your Triodos Bank experience — movement building, hyperlocalism, ethical purpose. The energy that drives the most effective community organisations is the same energy Thrive is trying to build into its fundraising. You have seen that work. You want to help Thrive build it.

You also noted something specific: local charities like Thrive have a rare acquisition advantage right now, because donors are increasingly disconnected from large national charities. The hyperlocal model is where the energy in individual giving is moving. You want to build that.

What do you know about Thrive?

You know they are in their centenary year — 100 years of operating in Somerset. You know they are repositioning from statutory contract delivery toward mission-led community work with growing unrestricted fundraising.

You know their village agent model: 70 paid staff embedded across the county, running talking cafes, providing advice and crisis support. You know the centenary goal is 2,000 supporters, not income. You know the email lists are not consented for fundraising. You know Beacon CRM is in place. You know Matthew manages trusts and foundations himself and will scaffold that area.

The hyperlocal framing, the Triodos connection, the 'winning a local election' metaphor for community acquisition — none of that is in the job description. It is your own thinking. When they ask why Thrive, do not give a polished answer. Tell them what you told yourself: about hyperlocalism, about why local charities have a rare donor acquisition advantage right now, about why the village agent model is the right infrastructure for a community fundraising movement.

Eight years of IG and legacy programme management — say it that way

You have managed a £1–1.2m individual giving and legacy programme for nearly eight years. You built Hft's first digital legacy programme from scratch. You steward 140+ pledgers. You manage multi-channel direct mail, digital campaigns, a community lottery, and legacy acquisition — and external agencies and suppliers throughout.

Do not describe this apologetically. Eight years of programme management at this scale is substantive experience. Lead with what you built and what you manage — not with how long you have been there.

When your line manager went on long-term sick, you stepped into Hft's legacy administration programme without warning. Complex estates, solicitors, property sales, restricted and unrestricted income, year-end audit requirements. You self-enrolled on specialist training immediately. You covered four months of administration while continuing active fundraising. You managed approximately £300,000 of legacy income through to year-end.

This is your strongest evidence that you can pick up new disciplines fast under pressure. Use it whenever the conversation turns to learning agility or how you would handle parts of the role outside your expertise.

Your Somerset roots are genuine — use them

You were schooled in Wells and Bruton. No other candidate in this process has genuine Somerset roots. You do not need to manufacture a connection. You have one. Mention it naturally when the conversation turns to geography or community understanding.

Areas to Prepare

Find your personal cause connection before you walk in

The question about genuine cause connection is coming. You have spent nearly eight years working alongside people with learning disabilities and their families. You have managed legacies from grieving families. You have seen services close. There is something in those eight years that moved you and changed how you worked. Find that before 18 June.

If you cannot find a single Hft moment, your Triodos experience is available — building a food waste movement, working with FareShare Bristol, the energy of connecting communities around a shared purpose. Frame it as the moment you understood why community connection produces better fundraising. That is directly relevant to Thrive.

Your best thinking is conversational — let the room know that is how you work

You are more fluent and more specific when the interview is a conversation than when it is structured questions. If you feel the format becoming rigid, it is fine to say: 'Can I give you a specific example to illustrate that?' and then broaden from the example. That is a more natural frame for how you think and Matthew will respond to it better.

Preparing your acquisition business case

When asked about your first acquisition spend, draw on the local press and hyperlocal channel thinking you described so clearly in your preparation. Something like: 'My first proposal would be a press insert campaign in Somerset-based local titles and parish magazines — channels that would not work for most charities but work for Thrive specifically because of the hyperlocal audience overlap. I would target three to five titles covering areas with high village agent activity. At approximately £X per insert, a 1% response rate at £10/month regular giving would break even within Y months. I would propose this as a six-week test before any wider rollout — the value is as much in the audience data as in the immediate response rate.'

Practice saying that out loud. You do not need exact numbers. You need the structure: channel rationale, target area logic, cost, projected response, break-even, and evaluation plan.
