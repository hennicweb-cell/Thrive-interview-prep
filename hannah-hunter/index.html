About Thrive

Who they are

Thrive is the operating name of the Community Council for Somerset — a charity with over 100 years of history (2026 is their centenary year). Registered at Fitzwarren House, Queen Street, Taunton. Charity number 1069260.

They have historically operated through contracts with Somerset County Council and are now actively repositioning — moving from contract delivery toward a more mission-focused model with growing unrestricted fundraising income. This is a genuine and significant pivot.

Their belief: everyone in Somerset should be able to live a healthy, independent and safe life and be part of a thriving community.

What they do — services and the village agent model

Thrive's work covers three areas: individuals and families (debt advice, fuel poverty support, mental health signposting); community buildings and places (training for people running local community spaces); and diverse and displaced communities (support for minority ethnic communities and refugees).

Their 70 village agents are paid staff — not volunteers — embedded across Somerset. They visit people at home, run talking cafes, provide advice and crisis support, and connect people to services. The FM's job is strategic enablement — building fundraising culture with the agents, designing toolkits, creating data capture systems — not operational delivery at events.

The fundraising picture

Thrive's income is almost entirely statutory contracts and grants. A very small number of existing regular givers. The real gap is donor acquisition. The centenary goal is 2,000 actively interested supporters by year end — not donors yet. 100 regular givers by year end would be welcome but is not a hard target.

There is no fixed acquisition budget. Every spend must be proposed with a clear rationale: channel, likely response, cost per supporter, break-even at regular giving level. Email lists exist (a few thousand contacts) but are not consented for fundraising — Thrive must not fundraise from current clients. Beacon CRM is in place.

Culture, values and the growth trajectory

Thrive genuinely lives its values: kindness and compassion, honesty and transparency, quality and diversity. Family-friendly, flexible, and an environment where it is okay to take risks and fail. Increasingly mission-focused — frontline staff will notice immediately whether a fundraiser genuinely cares.

If the FM performs well in year one, a digital fundraising officer follows in year two and the FM grows into head of fundraising. This is a builder role with a genuine career trajectory.

Areas to Prepare

The acquisition business case — add commercial structure to your thinking

Your acquisition instinct is events-first and warm-audience focused — which is consistent and logical given your career. The gap is commercial framing: cost, projected response rate, break-even at regular giving level. Matthew needs to hear that you can construct a proposal, not just a plan.

Before the interview, prepare one structured proposal you can deliver confidently. Draw on what you know from both Rowcroft and ECOE: 'My first proposal would be to use Thrive's centenary tea party events as paid acquisition events — marketing them to the wider community beyond existing service users through print leaflets in GP surgeries, community noticeboards and village halls, plus a small Facebook campaign targeting the local area. Budget of approximately £X for a defined test geography. At a 2% sign-up rate per event, with post-event personal conversations to understand motivations, I would project Y new consented contacts per event. At £10/month regular giving, break-even is around Z months. I would report back after the first three events before proposing a wider rollout.'

You do not need exact numbers. You need the structure: channel, target audience, budget, projected response, break-even, evaluation point. Practice saying it out loud.

Address the contractor question yourself — do not wait to be asked

Matthew is suspicious of contractors — he said so directly. You have a clear and honest answer: children, flexibility, now settled. But raise it yourself rather than waiting. Something like: 'I know a contractor background can raise questions about commitment. For me it was a practical choice when my children were young — they are now 10 and 13, I am settled back in the South West, I am looking to buy a home, and I want to be somewhere for several years and watch it grow. That is why this role matters to me.' Getting ahead of it shows confidence, not defensiveness.

Somerset — be specific about your West Country connection

You have no Somerset connection beyond Devon and going there regularly. Do not over-claim it. What you can say honestly is: you have lived in Devon since you were 20, these are your communities, and the rural South West is the context in which you do your best work. Your NZ experience — building fundraising relationships in dispersed, stoic, hard-to-reach rural communities — is directly transferable. That is a stronger answer than trying to manufacture a Somerset tie you do not have.
