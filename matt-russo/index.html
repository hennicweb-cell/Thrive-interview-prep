About Thrive

Who they are

Thrive is the operating name of the Community Council for Somerset — a charity with over 100 years of history (2026 is their centenary year). Registered at Fitzwarren House, Queen Street, Taunton. Charity number 1069260.

They have historically operated through contracts with Somerset County Council and are now actively repositioning — moving from contract delivery toward a more mission-focused model with growing unrestricted fundraising income. This is a genuine and significant pivot.

Their belief: everyone in Somerset should be able to live a healthy, independent and safe life and be part of a thriving community.

What they do — services and the village agent model

Thrive's work covers three areas: individuals and families (debt advice, fuel poverty support, mental health signposting); community buildings and places (training for people running local community spaces); and diverse and displaced communities (support for minority ethnic communities and refugees).

Their 70 village agents are paid staff — not volunteers — embedded across Somerset. They visit people at home, run talking cafes, provide advice and crisis support, and connect people to services. The FM's job is strategic enablement — building fundraising culture with the agents, designing toolkits, creating data capture systems — not operational delivery at events.

The fundraising picture

Thrive's income is almost entirely statutory contracts and grants. A very small number of existing regular givers. The real gap is donor acquisition. The centenary goal is 2,000 actively interested supporters by year end — not donors yet. 100 regular givers by year end would be welcome but is not a hard target.

There is no fixed acquisition budget. Every spend must be proposed with a clear rationale: channel, likely response, cost per supporter, break-even at regular giving level. Email lists exist (a few thousand contacts) but are not consented for fundraising — Thrive must not fundraise from current clients. Beacon CRM is in place.

Culture, values and the growth trajectory

Thrive genuinely lives its values: kindness and compassion, honesty and transparency, quality and diversity. Family-friendly, flexible, and an environment where it is okay to take risks and fail. Increasingly mission-focused — frontline staff will notice immediately whether a fundraiser genuinely cares.

If the FM performs well in year one, a digital fundraising officer follows in year two and the FM grows into head of fundraising. This is a builder role with a genuine career trajectory.

Why This Role — Preparing Your Answers
Why do you want this job?

Lead with what you built at RUHX — and why you have hit the ceiling. You arrived at a declining programme, built it from scratch to over £500k, and the organisation's structure does not give you the scope to go further. You have the skills. You need a bigger canvas.

Thrive gives you that canvas. A community-facing charity in Somerset, starting from near-zero on individual giving, in its centenary year, with 70 village agents as the infrastructure for community fundraising. You have done the RUHX build. You know what the first year looks like. This is the next version of that challenge, in a place and for a cause that matters personally to you.

The career trajectory is also right. Head of fundraising, building a team around you — that is where you want to go. Thrive is how you get there.

Why Thrive specifically?

This one needs to come from somewhere personal. Your cover letter has the most directly relevant personal disclosure: you have personally relied on local council services during a difficult time. You live in Somerset with your family. You have seen in a hospital environment what timely, accessible community support means to people who are struggling.

Do not save this for the end or bury it in the answer. Lead with it: 'The reason this opportunity means something to me is both professional and personal. Then move to the professional case.

You also observed something specific: Thrive's core fundraising challenge is awareness — people just do not know the service exists. That insight, and your plan for addressing it through donor acquisition, is directly what Matthew is looking for.

You produce your own materials — design, photography, digital ads. For a charity with a small, stretched marketing team, this is a significant day-one practical asset. Mention it when the conversation turns to resourcing.

Areas to Prepare

The village agent question — develop the conversion architecture

Your instinct to survey the agents first and be lean is right. Develop it further: 'Three months listening — understand what the agents deliver, what the communities they work in look like, what the blockers to fundraising conversations are. Alongside that I design a data capture mechanism for the centenary events: a QR code linking to a Beacon sign-up form, or a simple paper sign-up with consent language, so every event produces usable contacts. I brief the agents on how to introduce Thrive's work without making a hard ask — warm connection first, supporter relationship second. The system runs without me being present. That is how you convert 100 events into a growing supporter base.'

Preparing your acquisition business case

When asked about your first acquisition spend, draw on your RUHX experience specifically. Something like: 'My first proposal would be a door drop to postcode hotspot areas — areas with high village agent activity and known community need. I would design the drop myself, which keeps the cost down. At RUHX I took door drop income from £20k to £80k by layering Facebook geo-targeting on top — I would propose the same approach here. Test a defined Somerset geography with a £500-800 door drop, measure response, then propose a matched Facebook campaign to the same postcodes. The combined cost is low; the data you generate is high value. At £10/month regular giving, break-even is within the year.'

The RUHX reference makes it real — not a theoretical proposal. Practice saying it out loud.
